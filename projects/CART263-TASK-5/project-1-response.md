After looking through Ben and Pansy’s project, I was impressed with how it turned out. They took music from a famous artist who tragically passed away (Wowaka) and managed to turn it into this super smooth and interesting waveform type graphic. The program displayed different shapes depending on the song, like a heart for example, and it reacted to the music as it played. They also added a volume slider in the corner which was honestly pretty impressive to see functioning so well with everything else going on. Overall, the project definitely had a very deep meaning to it, and it was very well-made from start to finish. When you look at the coding aspect of it, I was personally very intrigued by the reactive display and how it all tied together. Looking through their code, I was very impressed at the complexity of it all, especially since it was all very well formatted and actually easy to read even with the advanced stuff in there. It seems like they used a lot of JavaScript features I’ve never even heard of before. I noticed the line “analyser.getByteTimeDomainData(dataArray)” in there while I was scrolling through, and I wouldn’t even know where to start when looking at something like that. It’s wild how they can take raw data from a sound file and map it to a visual shape so interestingly. The song/page with the heart shape was a great touch because of the context it had with the artist they chose. It didn't just feel like a random math equation, it felt like the music was actually breathing or in this case beating like a heart. Usually, when you see music visualizers, they are just basic bars or lines, but they went the extra mile to make it a symbolic shape. The way the heart would pulse more intensely during the louder parts of the song really showed how much effort went into the math behind the visuals, it wasn’t just random. Seeing the code for that was a bit overwhelming because of the logic required to keep the shape intact while pieces of it were constantly changing size/position, but they handled it in a way that looked very professional. I also thought the volume slider was a nice touch because it shows they were thinking about the user experience and not just the art. A lot of times in these types of projects, people forget the basic controls, but having that slider in the corner made it feel like a real, finished application. It’s those small details that really made the project stand out to me. Even though the JavaScript looked super complex to a beginner like me, seeing it work in real-time was quite inspiring. It makes me want to learn more about you can use sound as an interactive feature in these projects. To conclude it all, Ben and Pansy created a meaningful, fascinating project that gave me some interesting thoughts on JavaScript, and I’d like to incorporate some of the features they used into a future project of my own.
